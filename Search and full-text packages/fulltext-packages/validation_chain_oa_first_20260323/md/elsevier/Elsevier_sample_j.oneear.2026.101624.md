Climate extremes pose severe threats to vegetation structure and function. Vegetation dynamics are shaped by both concurrent climate conditions and legacy effects from past vegetation states, yet their relative roles in driving seasonal extreme negative anomalies in vegetation growth (NEGs) remain unclear. Here, we analyze satellite-derived vegetation data from 2000 to 2022 to attribute seasonal NEGs to climate extremes and past vegetation states. Approximately 60% of seasonal NEGs coincide with climate extremes. Cold extremes dominate vegetation reductions in boreal regions across all seasons, whereas in temperate regions, drivers shift from spring cold extremes to summer and autumn droughts or heatwaves. Independent of climate extremes, biological carryover effects account for >10% of seasonal NEGs and increase the likelihood of prolonged vegetation stress, with ∼23% of spring NEGs persisting into at least one subsequent season. Our findings suggest that biological legacies extend the impact of short-term climate extremes, complicating predictions of ecosystem responses to climate change.

These carryover effects extend the duration of severe seasonal vegetation reductions, particularly in semi-arid regions.

Terrestrial vegetation dynamics are a fundamental component of the Earth system, regulating the exchange of global carbon, water, and energy between the land surface and the atmosphere through biogeochemical and biophysical processes, 1 , 2 , 3 thereby exerting a critical influence on climate mitigation and the sustainability of ecosystem services. 4 , 5 In the Northern Hemisphere, vegetation activity follows marked seasonal cycles, including leaf emergence, growth, and senescence as well as changes in photosynthetic capacity, which are tightly influenced by seasonal variations in solar radiation, temperature accumulation, and water availability. 6 , 7 Variations in these seasonal drivers determine the timing and length of the growing season 8 , 9 , 10 , 11 and regulate ecosystem productivity, carbon sequestration, and land-atmosphere feedback. 12 , 13 , 14 , 15 , 16 , 17 While modest climatic fluctuations may lead to relatively small adjustments in vegetation activity, extreme climatic events (climate extremes [CEs]) can trigger pronounced reductions in vegetation activity, referred to as extreme negative anomalies in vegetation growth (NEGs). 18 , 19 , 20 Vegetation responses to such extremes may vary across seasons, as they are governed by distinct eco-physiological processes, 21 , 22 including tree water status (e.g., leaf and stem water potential), 23 , 24 carbon balance (e.g., photosynthesis, growth, and respiration), 25 and non-structural carbohydrate dynamics. 26 Moreover, climatic conditions in one season can generate “memory” effects (or so-called legacy effects) that influence growth in subsequent seasons by altering physiological functioning or environmental conditions. 27 , 28 , 29 , 30

Seasonal NEGs are frequently associated with concurrent CEs, defined here as the simultaneous occurrence of one or more CEs within a single season. 31 These extremes impose direct physiological constraints on vegetation dynamics. 18 For instance, drought disrupts water transport within the soil-plant-atmosphere continuum. 32 To maintain leaf water potential, plants partially close their stomata, suppressing photosynthesis and reducing carbon uptake. 29 Under prolonged drought, continued water loss may lead to hydraulic failure and the depletion of non-structural carbohydrate reserves, 24 , 26 potentially causing vegetation mortality. 33 Extreme precipitation can result in prolonged waterlogging, which hampers effective oxygen and nutrient absorption by roots and obstructs plant growth. 34 Heatwaves may result in major photosynthesis reductions or even shutdown, 35 while extreme cold can delay vegetation development or cause frost damage to plant tissues. 36 Despite well-documented impacts of individual CEs, less attention has been paid to whether the dominant climatic drivers of vegetation NEGs may shift across seasons. Most previous studies aggregated impacts over the entire growing season, 37 , 38 thereby obscuring seasonal differences in vegetation vulnerability and response mechanisms from spring to autumn. Identifying seasonal shifts in the key drivers of NEGs is therefore critical for improving accurate attribution of vegetation extremes and understanding how compound and legacy effects propagate through the annual cycle. 1 , 39 , 40

Beyond the impacts of concurrent CEs, increasing evidence from satellite-based studies reported that vegetation itself can propagate stress across seasons. 41 , 42 , 43 There are two key vegetation-mediated mechanisms related to these inter-seasonal linkages, the first of which is the carryover effect, whereby a negative vegetation anomaly persists into a subsequent season. This can occur when pre-season stress depletes carbon reserves or induces lasting physiological damage, such as impaired root or xylem function, thereby constraining recovery even after favorable conditions return. 41 The second mechanism is structural overshoot, 29 , 42 whereby positive vegetation anomalies and excessive growth in one season deplete stored carbon, nutrients, or water, increasing vulnerability and resulting in reduced growth in the following season. These inter-seasonal connections in vegetation dynamics have important ecological and carbon-cycle implications, as they can suppress carbon sequestration beyond the duration of a single extreme event, alter ecosystem stability, and reduce resilience to subsequent CEs. 28 , 44 , 45 Despite their significance, vegetation carryover effects and structural overshoot remain largely overlooked in attribution analyses of vegetation extremes, which typically focus on concurrent meteorological drivers. 37 , 38 , 46 , 47 Incorporating vegetation preconditions, defined here as the state of vegetation inherited from previous seasons, together with seasonal CEs, directly addresses this knowledge gap. This integration is important for quantifying carbon uptake during NEGs, characterizing post-event recovery trajectories and improving Earth system model predictions of carbon-cycle dynamics relevant to regional and national climate risk management. 48 , 49 , 50

Here, we identify the drivers of seasonal NEGs across northern mid- and high-latitude regions (23.5° N–70° N) during spring, summer, and autumn from 2000 to 2022, using the kernel normalized difference vegetation index (kNDVI). Our study region includes nearly all ecosystem types outside the tropics and exhibits pronounced seasonal variation in vegetation growth. 51 Over recent decades, these northern ecosystems have experienced widespread greening and have acted as prominent carbon sinks 52 ; however, this trend has stagnated in some regions, posing challenges to terrestrial carbon sequestration and ecosystem resilience. 53 Using coincidence analysis, 54 , 55 we assess the contribution of concurrent CEs and vegetation preconditions from the previous season to NEGs in spring, summer, and autumn. Specifically, our research objectives are to (1) identify the main and dominant factors of concurrent CE events responsible for NEGs in spring, summer, and autumn; (2) quantify the contributions of vegetation preconditions from the previous season, including carryover effects and structural overshoot, to NEGs, and how this interacts with extreme meteorological forcings; and (3) investigate the underlying mechanisms behind these identified climatic and biotic factor variations across seasons and regions.

We first identified the NEGs during spring (March-April-May [MAM]), summer (June-July-August [JJA]), and autumn (September-October-November [SON]) over the period 2000–2022, then assessed the spatial distributions and intensity of seasonal vegetation extremes across seasons. At each location, corresponding to a grid size of 0.1° × 0.1°, NEGs were determined using the local 15th percentile of seasonal average kNDVI anomalies in the detrended time series over the recent 23 years as the threshold, which corresponds to the smallest three values (see methods ). To account for spatial differences in variability, the intensity of these extreme anomalies was further normalized by the local standard deviation (SD). In spring, more than 60% of the study region experienced moderate NEGs, defined by anomalies ranging between −1 and −1.5 SD (where 1 SD reflects the magnitude of local inter-annual variability) ( Figure 1 A). As the seasons progressed into summer, NEGs substantially intensified, with severe anomalies (i.e., exceeding −1.5 SD) affecting more than 80% of the study region. This intensification was particularly pronounced in western and central North America, western Europe, and Siberia ( Figure 1 B). From summer to autumn, the intensity of NEGs decreased, with the area affected by severe reductions shrinking by about 40% ( Figure 1 C). Grouping seasonal NEGs by biome shows that the exacerbated summer vegetation growth extremes, followed by a slight recovery in autumn, were found in boreal and temperate forests and croplands ( Figure 1 D). In contrast, subtropical and semi-arid biomes exhibited relatively consistent NEGs throughout the seasons, with subtropical biomes suffering consistently severe anomalies, while semi-arid biomes typically experienced moderate anomalies.

For all identified seasonal NEGs, we assessed their associated CEs and identified the dominant concurrent climatic drivers associated with these events. To facilitate a more robust attribution to the occurrence of NEGs, we used coincidence analysis and random shuffling tests. Together, these methods can quantify the likelihood of coincidence and reduce the risk of spurious attribution of NEGs to climatic drivers (see methods ). In spring, more than 60% of NEGs were linked to concurrent CEs ( Figure 2 A), with slightly higher coincidence rates (CRs) in regions above 45° N compared to lower latitudes ( Figure 2 D). In areas of high coincidence, two or three types of CEs were typically identified as primary drivers of spring NEGs, while mid-latitudes often experienced more complicated and compound CEs ( Figure 2 A). At 22% of mid-latitudes, more than five CEs contributed to spring NEGs ( Figures 2 A and 2E). During summer, the proportion of NEGs linked to concurrent CEs decreased slightly to ∼57% ( Figures 2 B and 2C). A clear latitudinal gradient regarding the number of CE drivers emerged in summer: at higher northern latitudes, multiple CEs contributed to NEGs, whereas mid-latitudes generally experienced only one or two CEs ( Figure 2 F). This latitudinal pattern was not observed in autumn, where autumn NEGs across all regions were associated with multiple CEs ( Figure 2 G).

To identify the dominant climatic driver at each location and season, we defined the dominant climate extreme (CE domi ) as the type of extreme with the highest coincidence rate with NEGs. As shown in Figures 2 H–2J, spatial patterns of CE domi varied significantly across seasons. In high-latitude regions, cold-related extremes (T−) consistently dominated NEGs throughout spring, summer, and autumn, despite multiple climate drivers being identified in summer. In temperate regions, CE domi exhibited a significant seasonal shift: cold extremes (T−) dominated in spring ( Figure 2 H), while warm to high vapor pressure deficit (VPD) drought-driven (T+ V+ W−) events were the most common in summer ( Figure 2 I). In autumn, the CE domi is much more heterogeneous, including cold extremes (T−), wet extremes (W+), and drought-related extremes (W−, V+ W−, or T+ V+ W−) ( Figure 2 I). Notably, temperate regions exhibiting this seasonal shift in CE domi coincided with areas experiencing severe summer vegetation growth reductions ( Figure 1 B). This suggests that compound CEs have more detrimental impacts on vegetation growth compared to individual CEs. To evaluate the potential influence of climate data uncertainty, particularly given that reanalysis data such as the European Centre for Medium-Range Weather Forecasts Reanalysis (ERA5) dataset can have higher uncertainties in regions with sparse in situ observations (e.g., high latitudes), we performed a parallel analysis using the observation-based Climatic Research Unit Time Series (CRU TS) and European Space Agency Climate Change Initiative (ESA CCI) soil moisture datasets. The results confirm that the spatial patterns, latitudinal gradients of CRs between NEGs and CEs, and the dominant CE factors remain consistent ( Figure S2 ).

Our findings are broadly consistent with previous studies of individual CEs while providing a more comprehensive seasonal assessment and accounting for a wider range of potential drivers, including compound events. Based on mean annual vegetation activity, cold extremes were identified as key drivers in boreal forests, while drought emerged as the dominant factor for NEGs in temperate forests. However, our analysis shows a more nuanced, seasonally varying pattern, in which the types of dominant CEs affecting vegetation activity can change even at the same location. In spring, cold extremes are the primary cause of vegetation reductions across the largest affected area, mainly due to delayed spring phenology as plants lower their sensitivity to avoid frost damage. 56 , 57 This effect extends to temperate forests as well. In contrast, autumn vegetation activity is influenced by additional factors, including decreased radiation, lower water availability, or surface soil frost. 58 , 59 During summer, compound events such as heatwaves, atmospheric droughts, and soil droughts are closely linked to vegetation declines, particularly in temperate and, notably, boreal forests. These declines are associated with summer temperatures exceeding optimal photosynthetic thresholds 60 as well as elevated VPD combined with low soil moisture, which triggers stomatal closure, conserving water but decreasing photosynthesis. 61 Summer heatwaves and droughts have been reported to induce tree mortality in temperate European forests, as observed in recent extreme events. 33

In addition to concurrent CEs, we investigated the contribution of vegetation precondition, defined as the extreme state of vegetation in the previous season to seasonal NEGs from spring, to summer, to autumn (see methods ). On average, nearly 11% of seasonal NEGs can be attributed solely to vegetation precondition ( Figures 3 A–3C ; except where indicated, the CR between NEGs and preconditioned NEGs or positive anomalies in vegetation growth [PEGs] all excluded concurrent CEs, and vice versa). Our results show that the contribution of vegetation precondition to spring NEGs (for which we use vegetation extremes during the previous autumn, rather than winter) is lower than in summer and autumn ( Figure 3 A), particularly for annual plants such as crops and grasses ( Figures 3 G and S3 ), as cross-year ecological legacies are generally weaker in spring and minimal for herbaceous vegetation. Nevertheless, these memory effects remain reasonable, representing a carryover impact where environmental conditions from the previous autumn, which directly shaped that season’s vegetation and may have induced climate feedback, subsequently influencing growth in the following spring. This phenomenon has linkage with established drought legacy effects, 5 , 28 , 62 driven by endogenous vegetation processes (e.g., hydraulic damage and carbon depletion) and exogenous environmental factors (e.g., deep soil moisture depletion). 28 A recent study supports this by demonstrating that drought-induced negative vegetation extremes in autumn can significantly delay spring green-up via exogenous memory mechanisms involving post-drought temperature, local climate, and drought characteristics. 63 The contribution of vegetation precondition varies by biome, exhibiting a latitudinal gradient, with higher CRs at lower latitudes, for summer and autumn NEGs ( Figure 3 G). For evergreen boreal forests, tundra, and croplands, the contribution remains relatively modest (around 10%), whereas temperate forests show stronger associations with concurrent CEs. Subtropical and semi-arid biomes, however, exhibit high contributions from vegetation precondition, in some regions displaying contributions that match or even exceed those of concurrent CEs (compare Figures 3 A–3C to Figures 2 A–2C). While intensive land-cover change could potentially confound the linkages, we conducted a comparison of CRs between regions with stable and changed land cover, confirming that the relative contributions of concurrent CEs and vegetation precondition are robust across seasons and biomes ( Figure S4 ).

Regarding the type of vegetation precondition, as shown in Figures 3 D–3F, vegetation carryover effects generally dominate over structural overshoot effects across most study regions. Specifically, negative vegetation extremes from the previous season account for over 80% of seasonal NEGs attributed solely to vegetation precondition in semi-arid and subtropical biomes and for more than 60% in cropland, temperate, and boreal forests (compare bar sizes in Figure 3 G). The contribution of carryover effects increases progressively from spring to autumn ( Figures 3 D–3F), particularly in the semi-arid biome ( Figure 3 G). Conversely, vegetation structural overshoot effects are less common, appearing most pronounced in tundra at high northern latitudes during summer, where positive spring vegetation extremes lead to NEGs in summer (greener shading in Figure 3 E). The low frequency of structural overshoot effects suggests that resource depletion due to excessive growth in one season is less likely to drive NEGs in the following season. Instead, positive growth anomalies tend to persist, benefiting subsequent seasonal vegetation growth. 29 , 38 , 64 Furthermore, we found that both vegetation precondition and concurrent seasonal CEs can jointly influence seasonal NEGs, with similar patterns of precondition effects observed regardless of the presence of concurrent CEs ( Figure S5 ).

Previous studies have mainly focused on the inter-annual variability of vegetation activity rather than extreme events, emphasizing the role of vegetation structural overshoot effects, particularly in regulating activity from spring to summer. These studies found that vegetation in summer is more likely to experience lagged stress as increased water demands coupled with soil water deficits from the late spring amplify the effects of heatwaves triggered by positive vegetation activity extremes in spring. 30 , 65 , 66 Here, we observed that although such structural events do occur, the more prevalent inter-seasonal “memory” impact on vegetation suppression extremes arises from vegetation carryover effects, particularly in water-limited regions such as semi-arid and subtropical biomes. This pattern likely reflects the limited adaptability of vegetation to drought, which makes it highly sensitive to water availability changes 67 while gradually adopting conservative water-use strategies. 68 , 69 Our finding of prevalent carryover effects during summer and autumn suggests that an extreme vegetation state in spring can propagate memory effects, potentially leading to a cascade of impacts across subsequent seasons.

To test this hypothesis, we assessed whether extreme vegetation states in one season can weaken vegetation and influence the likelihood of subsequent extreme events in the following seasons. This analysis evaluated the memory effects between vegetation extremes, whether driven by concurrent CEs, vegetation preconditions, or cases where the underlying drivers cannot be attributed. For completeness, we also included extreme PEGs, defined by the metric of the 85th percentile of seasonal average kNDVI anomalies. As shown in Figure 4 A, 21% of PEGs in spring carried over to at least one subsequent season (all light- and dark-green pathways in Figure 4 A). Similarly, approximately 23% of NEGs observed in spring persisted into summer (dark-red pathway and light-red pathways in the upper part of Figure 4 A), with several continuing into autumn (dark-red pathways in Figure 4 A), while an additional 16% of NEGs that emerged in summer extended into autumn (light-red pathways in the lower part of Figure 4 A). These cascading effects are particularly pronounced in semi-arid biomes ( Figure S6 ), where 38% of spring NEGs continued into summer, with 45% of those carrying over into autumn (and so affecting three seasons). An additional 24% of NEGs that originated in summer extended into autumn (red pathways in Figure 4 A). This again highlights the significance of carryover effects in seasonal vegetation dynamics, here suggesting that NEGs during one season can serve as early-warning indicators for further negative extreme events in subsequent seasons. Once a NEG occurs, the likelihood of further negative growth anomalies in subsequent seasons may increase, potentially enhancing the impact of any extreme meteorological conditions in those next seasons.

We further confirmed that the likelihood of carryover effects from NEGs across seasons may relate to vegetation recovery capacity. Such vegetation recovery capacity was quantified by comparing kNDVI anomalies of NEGs with adjacent “normal” grids that did not experience NEGs (considering only paired grids in which neither suffered from CEs in the following seasons—see above). Regions with pronounced carryover effects correspond to areas of low vegetation recovery capacity, indicating a diminished capacity to return to a normal state. Low vegetation recovery capacity is particularly evident in xeric shrublands of North America, the Mediterranean, and central Asia, especially during autumn ( Figure 4 D), whereas this pattern is broadly absent in spring ( Figure 4 B). The limited recovery capacity in autumn might result from vegetation experiencing extreme negative anomalies in summer (often due to drought), leading to physiological damage such as carbon depletion, hydraulic failure, carbon allocation shifts, or even mortality, with full recovery potentially requiring several months. 24 , 26 The duration of recovery may depend on the severity of the NEGs, 70 , 71 the phenological stage when NEGs occur, 72 , 73 and the climatic conditions following the NEGs. 71 , 74 , 75

In contrast, croplands exhibited relatively high recovery capacity ( Figure 4 E), likely due to active human management practices. Furthermore, biomes characterized by high water availability and lower levels of human disturbance, such as sub-Arctic zones (including northeastern Siberia and northwestern United States) and the southeastern Tibetan Plateau, also demonstrated high recovery capacity ( Figure 4 C). These regions also exhibited vegetation structural overshoot effects during the summer ( Figure 3 G, green bar for Boreal/Alpine), which can be explained by two factors. First, vegetation in these areas, dominated by angiosperms and oak species, has strong recovery capacity and enhanced acclimation responses (i.e., low sensitivity to environmental stresses), largely due to the anatomy of their xylem and efficient whole-plant allocation strategies. 21 Second, climate stressors from the previous season may create more favorable conditions for subsequent growth, effectively producing positive overshoot effects. For example, spring NEGs often correspond to delayed snowmelt, but, once warming occurs, the increased water availability can promote summer vegetation growth ( Figures 3 B and S7 ). 76 , 77 These processes might explain some of the lower NEG values in Figures 3 B and 3C. However, it is important to note that such positive overshoot effects are limited to certain regions. Cascading reductions in vegetation growth across seasons, particularly in vulnerable areas, remain a significant concern.

In summary, we conducted a detection and attribution analysis of seasonal NEGs in Northern Hemisphere vegetation during spring, summer, and autumn from 2000 to 2022. Unlike annual NEGs, which are largely attributed to CEs, only about 60% of seasonal NEGs were associated with concurrent CEs. The dominant drivers of these concurrent CEs varied across seasons: cold extremes played a dominant role in spring, shifting to compound heatwave and drought events in summer, especially in temperate regions, while autumn NEGs were driven by a more diverse set of CEs, including cold-, wet-, and drought-related events. Importantly, we found that the negative extreme state of vegetation in the previous season exhibits a biological carryover effect on subsequent NEGs. This carryover effect is particularly pronounced in summer and autumn, reflecting the limited recovery capacity of vegetation following previous extreme negative states. As a result, approximately 23% of NEGs observed in spring persisted into summer, and an additional 21% of summer NEGs extended into autumn. In semi-arid biomes, carryover effects were especially strong, with 38% of spring NEGs persisting into at least one subsequent season.

These findings highlight that biological carryover can extend the impacts of short-term CEs or disturbances, leading to persistent reductions in vegetation growth. Accounting for these effects is critical for predicting future vegetation dynamics and carbon-cycle responses under changing climates. Current dynamic global vegetation models often inadequately represent biological carryover effects, 19 which may contribute to their poor performance in simulating extreme vegetation anomalies. Integrating high-frequency monitoring of vegetation activity and CEs can improve our understanding of these complex interactions, offering valuable insights for developing dynamic and targeted climate adaptation and mitigation strategies informed by vegetation activity. Overall, it is critical to consider both current climatic conditions and the past states of vegetation when assessing ecosystem health and resilience in the face of ongoing climate change.

Gridded climate data, including hourly 2 m air temperature, 2 m dewpoint temperature, total precipitation, and soil moisture data, were obtained from the ERA5 78 dataset. These data have a spatial resolution of 0.1° × 0.1°, covering the period from 1950 to 2024. To derive monthly values, the hourly data were aggregated to monthly averages. For soil moisture, data are provided at four layers with depths of 0–7 cm, 7–28 cm, 28–100 cm, and 100–289 cm. Two soil moisture metrics were derived from the four-layer data: (1) a depth-weighted average integrating four layers to a fixed 3 m profile, and (2) a soil moisture specifically at the vegetation rooting depth by the use of the global root depth dataset of Stocker et al. 79 A sensitivity analysis confirmed that the detection of soil-moisture-related extremes was robust to the choice of soil moisture metric ( Figure S8 ). Daily VPD (kPa) was calculated as (Equation 1) VPD = 0.61078 × ( e T dew × 17.27 T dew + 237.3 − e T × 17.27 T + 237.3 ) , where T dew is the daily 2 m dewpoint temperature (K), and T is the daily 2 m air temperature (K). The daily VPD values were then aggregated to produce monthly averages.

Monthly frost days were obtained from the CRU TS v.4.07 80 dataset, which has a spatial resolution of 0.5° × 0.5° and covers the period from 1900 to 2022. They were resampled to a spatial resolution of 0.1° × 0.1° using nearest-neighbor interpolation to match the spatial resolutions of the other climate data.

Vegetation growth was characterized using the kNDVI, which accounts for higher-order spectral channel relations. 81 This vegetation index is more sensitive to vegetation activity and less prone to saturation compared to the traditional NDVI, demonstrating robust performance across various applications. 82 The kNDVI was calculated as (Equation 2) kNDVI = tanh ⁡ NIR − red 2 σ 2 , where σ represents the sensitivity of kNDVI to areas of sparse or dense vegetation. When σ = 0.5(NIR + red), which is recommended to show a good compromise between accuracy and simplicity, 81 the formula for calculating kNDVI can be simplified as (Equation 3) kNDVI = tanh NDVI 2 .

Based on Equation 3 , we calculated the kNDVI using NDVI data from the MCD43A4 Nadir Bidirectional Reflectance Distribution Function (BRDF)-Adjusted Reflectance (NBAR) dataset. 83 This product corrects for view-angle effects in surface reflectance, providing a stable and consistent data source with a spatial resolution of 463 m and a temporal resolution of 1 day, covering the period from 2000 to the present. Daily data were quality controlled using “Fmask” to remove pixels or time affected by clouds, snow, or water. The monthly kNDVI values were then aggregated to a spatial resolution of 0.1° × 0.1° by averaging the grid values.

Biome classification data were obtained from the RESOLVE Ecoregions map (Ecoregions2017), 84 an updated version of the world terrestrial ecoregions map. 85 This map categorizes terrestrial ecosystems into 14 distinct biomes and is crafted from over a century of biogeographical research and extensive field surveys, providing a more accurate representation of regional biomes compared to biogeographic units derived from parametric methods. We rasterized the polygon layer into 0.1° × 0.1° grid data based on biome classification, using ArcGIS 10.4 (ESRI, Redlands, CA, USA). Land-cover-type data were obtained from the ESA CCI annual land-cover maps (Copernicus Climate Change Service, Climate Data Store, 2019), classified according to the UN FAO’s Land Cover Classification System (LCCS). With an original spatial resolution of 300 m, these maps cover the period from 1992 to 2022. For this study, we selected the 2022 land-cover map and resampled it to a spatial resolution of 0.1° × 0.1° using the modal value within each target grid cell to match the spatial resolution of climate data.

Based on the Ecoregions map and land-cover map, the natural biomes of northern mid- and high-latitude regions were categorized into five major categories: boreal and alpine ecosystems (consisting of Boreal Forest, Tundra, and Montane Grasslands & Shrublands), temperate ecosystems (consisting of Temperate Broadleaf & Mixed Forests, Temperate Conifer Forests, and Temperate Grasslands, Savannas & Shrublands), subtropical ecosystems (consisting of Tropical & Subtropical Coniferous Forests, Tropical & Subtropical Dry Broadleaf Forests, and Tropical & Subtropical Grasslands), and semi-arid ecosystems (consisting of Mediterranean Forests, Woodlands & Scrub, Desert & Xeric Shrublands, and Tropical & Subtropical Grasslands, Savannas & Shrublands). Additionally, due to the significant influence of human activities on vegetation growth in agricultural ecosystems, areas classified as cropland in the land-cover map were categorized separately ( Figure S1 ). We excluded grid cells with an annual average NDVI < 0.1 over 23 years or those classified as bare areas in the land-cover map.

Our study region encompasses northern mid- and high-latitude areas (23.5° N–70° N). Seasonal averages for both kNDVI and climate data were calculated as three-month averages for spring (MAM), summer (JJA), and autumn (SON). Attribution analysis was conducted separately for these three seasons, while winter (December–February), a non-growing season for vegetation, was excluded from our analysis. To identify vegetation growth extremes, the seasonal kNDVI time series from 2000 to 2022 were first detrended with a linear fit. We then identified high (PEGs) and low (NEGs) seasonal kNDVI extremes using the grid-wise 85th and 15th percentiles of the detrended seasonal kNDVI averages. Note that percentile-based thresholds were calculated for each pixel and for each season individually. This ensures that vegetation states are only compared within the same season and location. The same method and threshold were applied to climate data to identify seasonal CEs. We selected this threshold to balance the number of events available for analysis and their degree of extremity.

We identified five types of individual seasonal CEs: cold extremes (TMP−), heatwaves (TMP+), extreme atmospheric dryness (VPD+), extreme soil dryness (WA−), and extreme wetness (WA+). Following Yang et al., 37 TMP− is defined when negative anomalies of seasonal average or minimum daily temperature fall below the 15th percentile or when positive anomalies of the number of frost days exceed the 85th percentile. TMP+ is defined by positive anomalies of seasonal average or maximum daily temperature exceeding the 85th percentile. VPD+ refers to positive anomalies of seasonal average or maximum daily VPD exceeding the 85th percentile. WA− is defined when negative anomalies of average precipitation or soil moisture fall below the 15th percentile, while WA+ represents positive anomalies of average precipitation or soil moisture above the 85th percentile. Additionally, NEGs can also arise from multiple simultaneous CEs, referred to as compound CEs. A total of six common types of compound CEs were identified: TMP− & WA−, TMP− & WA+, TMP+ & WA−, TMP+ & WA+, VPD+ & WA−, and VPD+ & TMP+ & WA−. Other compound CEs were excluded due to their low coincidence with seasonal extreme reductions in vegetation growth (<1%). Stricter thresholds of the 90th and 10th percentiles were applied to both vegetation and climate data, confirming the robustness of our main results ( Figures S9–S11A ). We also conducted a parallel analysis using an SD-based approach and found that the results remained consistent with those based on percentiles ( Figures S12 and S13 ).

Event coincidence analysis was employed to investigate the major influencing factors for NEGs, including contributions from concurrent CEs and the vegetation preconditioning. This method evaluates the strength of the linkage between two events by calculating their CR. 54 , 55 Coincidence rates for each season were calculated within a 15 × 15 spatial moving window to increase the sample size and reduce the effect of outliers in individual grids ( Figure S14 ). On the one hand, the CRs between NEGs and the 11 types of CEs identified in the section “ identifying extreme events in vegetation growth and climate ” can be calculated as follows: (Equation 4) CR CEs & NEGs = Frequency CEs & NEGs Frequency NEGs × 100 % , where CR CEs&NEGs represents the coincidence rate of the CE and NEGs during 2000–2022, Frequency NEGs represents the frequency of NEGs, and Frequency CEs&NEGs represents the frequency at which the CEs and NEGs occur simultaneously.

The CE with the highest CR with NEGs among all 11 types of CEs (including both individual and compound CEs) is regarded as the dominant climate extreme driver (CE domi ) for NEGs. To further evaluate the statistical significance of the relationship between CEs and NEGs, we implemented a repeated coincidence analysis through random time shuffling. Specifically, we randomly shuffled the time series of climate data and recalculated the CRs with NEGs. This process was repeated 1,000 times. A t test was subsequently performed to compare the CRs derived from the actual data with observed time series against those obtained through reshuffled data. If the former was significantly higher at the 0.05 level, it indicated a meaningful relationship between the CEs and the observed vegetation reduction. This analysis allowed us to identify how many, and which specific, concurrent CEs serve as key drivers behind seasonal reductions in vegetation growth ( Figure S15 ). On the other hand, we also calculated the CRs of NEGs with preconditioned vegetation negative extremes and positive extremes to assess the contributions of carryover effects and structural overshoot to NEGs. This calculation was based on the assumption that pixels within a spatial window share similar characteristics with the central pixel. Although climatic and soil properties exhibit strong spatial autocorrelation, land cover can be more heterogeneous. To test the robustness of our results to this assumption, we conducted a sensitivity analysis that excluded pixels with land-cover types (from the RESOLVE Ecoregions map) differing from the central pixel. The results confirmed that the calculated contributions were weakly influenced by land-cover homogeneity ( Figures S16 and S17 ).

Vegetation recovery capacity refers to the ability of vegetation to recover from extreme negative anomaly states. 27 To quantify this capacity, we used a paired-difference approach that compares the current-season kNDVI anomalies (normalized by SD) of previously stressed pixels against those of “normal” pixels, thereby isolating the recovery signal from concurrent climatic influences. Specifically, to quantify vegetation recovery capacity from spring to summer (as shown in Figure S18 ), we calculated the difference between the summer kNDVI anomalies of a NEG grid and the average summer kNDVI anomalies of all normal grids within a 5 × 5 moving window centered on the NEG grids. A non-significant difference in these anomalies indicates a capacity for rapid recovery. To enhance the robustness of our assessment, we calculated this recovery metric for each season and then derived a multi-year average, thereby accounting for both seasonal and inter-annual variability. The choice of a 5 × 5 moving window balanced the need for geographically proximate grids with sufficient data for analysis. We confirmed the robustness of our results by performing the same analysis with 7 × 7 moving windows ( Figures S11 B–S11E). Furthermore, applying this method to grids with PEGs displayed opposite results ( Figure S7 ).
